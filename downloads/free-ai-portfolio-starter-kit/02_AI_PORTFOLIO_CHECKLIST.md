# AI Portfolio Checklist for Data Scientists

A strong AI/data science portfolio is not a project dump.

It should show:
- what kind of problems you understand
- how you think through technical tradeoffs
- how you explain complex work
- how your work connects to real-world use

## 1. Positioning

☐ Is your positioning clear in one glance?

☐ Can a visitor understand what kind of AI/data science work you do?

☐ Are you positioned broadly enough for future opportunities?

☐ Are you avoiding being trapped in only one domain unless that is your deliberate niche?

☐ Are you using specific language instead of generic words like “AI enthusiast” or “passionate problem solver”?

Good positioning examples:
- AI evaluation
- Applied AI
- LLM quality
- AI workflow design
- Real-world AI systems
- Technical writing for AI systems

## 2. Homepage

☐ Does your homepage explain your work quickly?

☐ Does it feel calm and credible?

☐ Does it avoid flashy AI visuals and buzzwords?

☐ Are your projects curated instead of dumped?

☐ Is there a clear path to your projects, writing, and contact page?

## 3. Projects

☐ Does each project explain the problem?

☐ Does each project explain why the problem was hard?

☐ Does each project explain your approach?

☐ Does each project mention evaluation, tradeoffs, or constraints?

☐ Does each project explain why the work mattered?

☐ Are you avoiding confidential names, internal metrics, and private company details?

## Public-safe sharing check

Before publishing company-related work, remove:

☐ client names  
☐ internal tool names  
☐ private metrics  
☐ exact architecture details  
☐ unreleased product details  
☐ sensitive data details  

You can usually keep:

☐ problem type  
☐ your role  
☐ method category  
☐ evaluation thinking  
☐ tradeoffs  
☐ lessons learned  

## 4. About page

☐ Does your About page tell a clear career story?

☐ Does it sound human without becoming too personal?

☐ Does it show depth without sounding stiff?

☐ Does it explain your technical interests clearly?

☐ Does it avoid sounding like a copied resume summary?

## 5. Writing / blog

☐ Does your writing section support your authority?

☐ Are your blogs connected to your actual thinking and expertise?

☐ Are your posts more than generic definitions?

☐ Do they explain why a concept matters in practice?

☐ Do they show your judgment, not just your knowledge?

## 6. LinkedIn alignment

☐ Does your LinkedIn headline match your website positioning?

☐ Does your Featured section include your website or best writing?

☐ Does your About section sound consistent with your website?

☐ Are your posts connected to your deeper work?

☐ Is your profile broad enough for consulting, writing, talks, or future roles?

## 7. Analytics

☐ Do you know how many people visit your portfolio?

☐ Do you know which page they visit most?

☐ Do you know whether people click from LinkedIn to your website?

☐ Do you know which blog posts get the most attention?

☐ Do you know whether people reach your Contact or Work With Me page?

☐ Do you review analytics before changing your website?

☐ Are you tracking signals beyond likes, such as saves, comments, DMs, website clicks, and time spent on pages?

## 8. Final quality check

☐ Does your website feel credible?

☐ Does it feel technically serious?

☐ Does it feel readable on mobile?

☐ Does it avoid overclaiming?

☐ Does it make someone want to read more?

## Quick 5-minute analytics check

After publishing or updating your portfolio, check:

☐ Which page got the most visits this week?

☐ Did any LinkedIn post bring website traffic?

☐ Did visitors reach your project, blog, or contact page?

☐ Which topic seems worth writing about again?
