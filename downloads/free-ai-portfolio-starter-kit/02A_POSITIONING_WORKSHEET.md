# AI Portfolio Positioning Worksheet

Use this before writing your homepage, LinkedIn headline, or project page.

Your positioning does not need to be perfect. It needs to help a reader understand what kind of problems you work on, what kind of thinking you bring, and why your work matters.

## 1. Your Core Direction

Fill this in quickly. Do not overthink the first draft.

I work on:

`[problem type / technical area]`

Using:

`[methods, tools, or systems]`

For:

`[audience, domain, team, or use case]`

So that:

`[practical outcome]`

Example:

I work on AI evaluation and language systems using NLP, LLM evaluation, error analysis, and workflow design for teams building real-world AI products, so that AI systems are more reliable, useful, and safe in practice.

## 3. Avoid Weak Positioning

Weak:

Data scientist passionate about AI and solving problems.

Stronger:

Data scientist working on applied AI, model evaluation, and real-world ML systems.

Weak:

AI enthusiast with experience in Python and machine learning.

Stronger:

AI professional focused on LLM evaluation, NLP workflows, and practical model reliability.

## 4. Draft Your One-Line Positioning

Use one of these structures.

Option A:

`I work on [technical area] for [audience/use case], with a focus on [specific strength].`

Option B:

`I help [audience/team] understand and improve [problem/system] using [methods].`

Option C:

`My work sits at the intersection of [theme 1], [theme 2], and [theme 3].`

## 5. Check Your Positioning

☐ Is it specific enough to sound real?

☐ Is it broad enough for future opportunities?

☐ Does it avoid generic words like “enthusiast” and “passionate”?

☐ Does it match the projects you actually want to show?

☐ Could the same line work on your website and LinkedIn?
